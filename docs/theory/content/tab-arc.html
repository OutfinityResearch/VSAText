Arc Tab

The Arc tab selects and displays the narrative arc template that guides your story structure. The chosen arc defines the beats your story should hit and their expected positions.

When to Use This Tab

Use the Arc tab early in story planning to select the structural template that best fits your narrative. This choice influences all subsequent planning.

What This Tab Does

The Arc tab provides:

  • Selection of narrative arc templates
  • Visual display of arc beats and positions
  • Beat descriptions and purposes
  • Tension curve visualization
  • Comparison between arc types

Available Arcs

Hero's Journey (12 Beats)

Origin: Joseph Campbell | Best For: Epic adventures, transformations

Beat Position Description
Ordinary World 8% Hero in their normal environment
Call to Adventure 12% Challenge or quest presented
Refusal of the Call 17% Hero hesitates or declines
Meeting the Mentor 22% Guide appears with wisdom
Crossing the Threshold 25% Hero commits and enters new world
Tests, Allies, Enemies 40% Challenges and new relationships
Approach to Inmost Cave 50% Preparing for major challenge
The Ordeal 55% Facing greatest fear, death/rebirth
Reward 65% Hero claims prize of the quest
The Road Back 75% Returning to ordinary world
Resurrection 85% Final test, transformation complete
Return with Elixir 95% Hero returns transformed

Three Act Structure (9 Beats)

Origin: Aristotle/Hollywood | Best For: General fiction, screenplays

Beat Position Description
Hook 2% Capture audience attention
Setup 8% Establish world and characters
Inciting Incident 12% Event that starts the story
Plot Point 1 25% End of Act 1, commitment
Rising Action 35% Complications increase
Midpoint 50% Major shift, stakes raised
Plot Point 2 75% All Is Lost moment
Climax 88% Final confrontation
Resolution 95% New equilibrium

Save the Cat (15 Beats)

Origin: Blake Snyder | Best For: Commercial fiction, detailed plotting

Key beats include: Opening Image, Theme Stated, Setup, Catalyst, Debate, Break Into Two, B Story, Fun and Games, Midpoint, Bad Guys Close In, All Is Lost, Dark Night of Soul, Break Into Three, Finale, Final Image.

Story Circle (8 Beats)

Origin: Dan Harmon | Best For: Character-focused stories

  1. You (Comfort Zone)
  2. Need (Want Something)
  3. Go (Enter Unfamiliar)
  4. Search (Adapt)
  5. Find (Get It)
  6. Take (Pay Price)
  7. Return
  8. Change (Capable of Change)

Kishotenketsu (4 Beats)

Origin: Japanese tradition | Best For: Stories without central conflict

  1. Ki (Introduction) - Introduce characters and setting
  2. Sho (Development) - Develop without complication
  3. Ten (Twist) - Unexpected turn or revelation
  4. Ketsu (Conclusion) - Reconciliation and harmony

Five Act / Freytag's Pyramid (5 Beats)

Origin: Freytag | Best For: Classical drama, tragedies

  1. Exposition
  2. Rising Action
  3. Climax (center)
  4. Falling Action
  5. Denouement

Seven-Point Structure (7 Beats)

Origin: Dan Wells | Best For: Novels with clear turning points

  1. Hook
  2. Plot Turn 1
  3. Pinch Point 1
  4. Midpoint
  5. Pinch Point 2
  6. Plot Turn 2
  7. Resolution

Chapter Arc (5 Beats)

Scope: Individual chapters

  1. Hook - Grab attention
  2. Goal - What character wants
  3. Conflict - Obstacles faced
  4. Turn - Unexpected development
  5. Cliffhanger - Unresolved tension

Scene Arc (3 Beats)

Scope: Individual scenes

  1. Goal - Character objective
  2. Conflict - Opposition
  3. Outcome - Success or failure

Choosing an Arc

If Your Story Is... Consider...
Epic adventure with transformation Hero's Journey
Character-driven personal growth Story Circle
Commercial/mainstream fiction Save the Cat
Classical tragedy Five Act
Slice of life, no central conflict Kishotenketsu
Novel with clear structure Seven-Point or Three Act
