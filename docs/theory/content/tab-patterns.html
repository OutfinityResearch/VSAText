Patterns Tab

The Patterns tab manages master plots and story patterns - proven narrative structures that have engaged audiences across cultures and centuries.

When to Use This Tab

Use the Patterns tab when choosing your story's fundamental plot type. Patterns provide tested frameworks for conflict, stakes, and resolution.

What This Tab Does

The Patterns tab helps you:

  • Browse master plot categories
  • Understand pattern structures and variations
  • Select patterns that fit your story
  • View examples from classic literature
  • Combine patterns for complexity

Master Plots (Tobias' 20 Plots)

Ronald Tobias identified 20 fundamental story patterns:

Pattern Core Dynamic Examples
Quest Hero searches for something valuable Lord of the Rings, Indiana Jones
Adventure Hero goes to new world, overcomes challenges Gulliver's Travels, Avatar
Pursuit Chase between pursuer and pursued Les Miserables, The Fugitive
Rescue Hero saves someone from danger Saving Private Ryan, Taken
Escape Hero breaks free from confinement The Shawshank Redemption, The Great Escape
Revenge Hero retaliates for wrong done The Count of Monte Cristo, Kill Bill
The Riddle Hero must solve mystery to survive Oedipus Rex, The Da Vinci Code
Rivalry Two characters compete for same goal Amadeus, The Prestige
Underdog Disadvantaged hero triumphs Rocky, Cinderella
Temptation Hero faces moral test Faust, The Devil's Advocate
Metamorphosis Physical or psychological transformation Beauty and the Beast, The Fly
Transformation Character changes through experience A Christmas Carol, The Shawshank Redemption
Maturation Coming of age journey Stand By Me, Harry Potter
Love Two people find each other Pride and Prejudice, When Harry Met Sally
Forbidden Love Love against social barriers Romeo and Juliet, Brokeback Mountain
Sacrifice Hero gives up everything for others A Tale of Two Cities, Armageddon
Discovery Hero finds truth about self or world The Sixth Sense, Chinatown
Wretched Excess Rise and fall through hubris Citizen Kane, The Great Gatsby
Ascension Rise from obscurity to greatness Forrest Gump, The King's Speech
Descension Fall from grace to ruin Macbeth, Breaking Bad

Booker's Seven Basic Plots

Christopher Booker's meta-patterns:

Plot Structure Examples
Overcoming the Monster Hero defeats threatening evil force Beowulf, Jaws, Star Wars
Rags to Riches Poor protagonist gains success Cinderella, Great Expectations
The Quest Hero journeys to find treasure Lord of the Rings, Raiders
Voyage and Return Hero visits strange land, returns changed Alice in Wonderland, Wizard of Oz
Comedy Confusion resolved, order restored Much Ado About Nothing
Tragedy Hero's flaw leads to downfall Macbeth, Breaking Bad
Rebirth Protagonist redeemed through experience A Christmas Carol, Beauty and the Beast

Pattern Combinations

Complex stories often combine patterns:

Combination Example
Quest + Transformation Lord of the Rings (Frodo's journey changes him)
Love + Forbidden Love Titanic (class barriers + tragedy)
Revenge + Descension Moby Dick (Ahab's obsession destroys him)
Escape + Maturation The Shawshank Redemption

Step-by-Step: Selecting Patterns

  1. Click "Select Pattern"
  2. Browse master plots by category
  3. Read pattern descriptions and examples
  4. Choose primary pattern
  5. Optionally add secondary patterns
  6. Review suggested themes and blocks
  7. Save selection

Metrics Impact

  • Pattern Completion: Does the story fulfill pattern expectations?
  • Beat Alignment: Are pattern-required beats present?
  • Resolution Satisfaction: Does ending match pattern type?

Best Practices

  1. Start with one pattern: Master it before combining
  2. Know your ending: Pattern implies resolution type
  3. Subvert thoughtfully: Breaking patterns can backfire
  4. Match to theme: Quest fits "growth", Tragedy fits "hubris"
  5. Study examples: Understand how classics use patterns
